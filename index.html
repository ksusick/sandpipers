Sandpipers

Sandpipers and Allies (Scolopacidae) consist of 34 different bird species that share a common ancestery and similar behaviors and habitats. They prefer shorelines such as coastal sandflats or wetlands, including estuaries and lakes. Sandpipers forage in mud, sand, or soil in search of invertebrates and other small prey.

Similar-looking Sandpipers of California

Species of Scolopacidae can be difficult to distinguish from one another because they may have similar body and bill shapes. In addition, its likely that they are being viewed from a distance as they feed along the shoreline, or are in flight. Listed below and briefly described are five relatively large-bodied members of Scolopacidae found in California that might be confused for one another yet can be told apart by certain distinct attributes if observed more carefully.

  • Long-billed Dowitcher
  • Willet
  • Marbled Godwit
  • Whimbrel
  • Long-billed Curlew

Long-billed Dowitcher

The Long-billed Dowitcher (Limnodromus scolopaceus) is the smallest of the sandpipers listed here, but has mottled gray winter plummage like the others and a stilt-like appearance. (The Short-billed Dowitcher isn't included in this list but they and the Long-billed Dowitcher were at one time considered the same species.) This species prefers fresh-water marshes and uses its straight long bill to feed on insects and aquatic invertebrates. A cool fact about the Long-billed Dowitcher is that their bills contain a high number of tactile receptors for locating prey. Its current status on the conservation watch list is least concern.

Willet

The Willet (Tringa semipalmata) prefers coastal shoreline and saltwater marshes during winter where it feeds primarily on crabs and other marine invertebrates. It has a straight bill like the Dowitcher but it is shorter and uses it differently by feeding at the surface. A cool fact about the Willet is that when a predator approaches a nest it will pretend to have a broken wing to lure it away. Its current status on the conservation watch list is least concern with populations declining.

Marbled Godwit

The Marbled Godwit (Limosa fedoa) has a sword-shaped bill that is slightly upturned, and is a good way for viewers to distinguish this bird from other sandpipers. It uses its bill to pierce deep into mudflats and sand to find aquatic invertebrates, vegetation, and even small fish. Its wintering grounds are coastal shoreline, including tidal flats of estuaries. A cool fact about the Marbled Godwit is that most populations breed in the northern Great Plains and feed mainly on plant tubers during migration. Its current status on the conservation watch list is least concern with populations declining.

Whimbrel

The Whimbrel (Numenius phaeopus) has a similar slender body shape and winter plummage to the Marbled Godwit, but can easily be distinguished by its long, down-curved bill. Whimbrels feed mosly on aquatic crabs and other marine invertebrates along tidal flats as wintering grounds. This includes estuaries and saltmarshes as well as rocky intertidal. A cool fact about the Whimbrel is that it has a migration route that stretches from nesting grounds in the Artic to as far south as South America during winter. Its current status on the conservation watch list is least concern with populations declining.

Long-billed Curlew

The Long-billed Curlew (Numenius americanus) has an even longer curved bill than the Whimbrel. It is the largest shorebird of North America (Herons and egrets aren't considered shorebirds). It uses its incredibly long bill to probe for marine invertebrates like crustaceans and insects along coastal mudflats including estuaries and marshes during winter. A cool fact about Long-billed Curlews is that its genus name, Numenius, means “of the new moon,” appropriately describing its slender, curved bill. Its current status on the conservation watch list is least concern.
